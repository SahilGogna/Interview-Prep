# Behavioural Interview Questions Guide

A practical guide put together from an ORU mentorship session, covering the most common behavioural questions, what interviewers are actually looking for, and how to structure your answers.

---

## Questions Covered in This Guide

1. Tell me about yourself.
2. How do you prioritise when you have multiple tasks on your plate?
3. Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a team member or stakeholder.
4. How would you convince a stakeholder or your manager to go with your approach?

---

## A Note on Behavioural Interviews

Behavioural interviews are not a mystery. There are roughly 10 to 15 patterns of questions that come up time and again, across companies and roles. The good news is that means you can prepare for almost everything in advance. The goal of this guide is to help you understand the thinking behind each question so you can answer it with confidence rather than just memorising a script.

One framework that works well across all behavioural questions is the STAR method: Situation, Task, Action, Result. Every answer you prepare should follow this arc. But beyond structure, what separates a good answer from a great one is storytelling. If your story is compelling, the small fumbles and pauses in between will not matter.

## Question 1: "Tell me about yourself."

### Why they ask it

This is almost always the first question, and it does more than just warm things up. The interviewer is using your answer to decide what they want to dig into for the rest of the conversation. They are looking for signals about your relevance to the role, your ability to communicate clearly, and whether you are someone they would enjoy working with.

### What the interviewer wants to hear

They want a focused, professional snapshot of who you are right now. Not your life story. Not a list of tools. Just a clear picture of your current work, a relevant highlight or two, and a small personal element that makes you memorable.

### How to answer it

Keep it to 30 to 60 seconds. Start with what you are doing now, then briefly mention your most relevant experience or a project that ties directly to the role you are applying for. End with something light, like a hobby or an interesting personal fact, that humanises you without being distracting.

A technique worth knowing is called interview seeding. At the start of your answer, you deliberately plant a seed, mentioning something you want the interviewer to ask you about. For example, if you are particularly proud of a project or a skill, weave it into your introduction in a way that invites a follow-up question. This way you are subtly steering the conversation toward ground you are comfortable on.

Before the interview, look up your interviewers on LinkedIn. If you notice any shared interests, you can naturally mention them in your introduction without explicitly saying you researched them. If you both love running, mention that you recently started training. The interviewer will often connect the dots themselves.

**What to avoid:**
- Starting from your high school or college history
- Listing tools you know one by one
- Speaking for more than 2 minutes
- Sounding like you memorised a script word for word

### Example

"I am currently a data analyst at [Company], where I focus on building dashboards and automating reporting workflows for the operations team. Before that, I spent two years at [Previous Company] working closely with cross-functional stakeholders on product analytics. One thing I have been exploring lately outside of work is using AI tools to speed up my day-to-day analysis, which has been pretty eye-opening. I also recently got into distance running, which keeps me grounded."

Notice how this answer is brief, relevant, and ends on a personal note that is easy to build a conversation around. It also plants a seed around AI tools that the interviewer might want to explore further.

## Question 2: "How do you prioritise when you have multiple tasks on your plate?"

### Why they ask it

This question is testing whether you can manage competing demands without falling apart or waiting for someone to tell you what to do. It also often leads to a follow-up: "But how do you actually decide what is more important?" Many candidates answer the first part fine and then get stuck on the follow-up.

### What the interviewer wants to hear

They want to see that you have a clear, repeatable framework for making decisions under pressure, and that you communicate proactively with the people who depend on you.

### How to answer it

Structure your answer around three things:

1. **Urgency** - How time-sensitive is this task? What happens if it slips by a day?
2. **Impact** - What is the effect of this task on the business or the team? A task can be urgent but low-impact, or low urgency but very high-impact. The combination of these two factors tells you where to focus first.
3. **Communication** - Once you have reprioritised, you loop in your stakeholders. You let them know what you are working on, what is being pushed, and when they can expect things.

This third part is what most candidates forget, and it is what separates someone who is just "busy" from someone who is genuinely reliable.

### Example

"When I have multiple things competing for my attention, I first assess each task on two dimensions: how urgent it is and what the business impact would be if it was delayed. A task might be urgent but low-impact, or the opposite. That combination tells me where to start. Once I have made that call, I communicate it. I reach out to the relevant stakeholders to let them know the order I am working in and give them a realistic timeline. That way nobody is left wondering where things stand."

## Question 3: "Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a team member or stakeholder."

### Why they ask it

This is one of the most revealing questions in any behavioural interview. The interviewer is not looking for a story where you were right and the other person was wrong. They are trying to understand whether you are someone who can navigate disagreement professionally, listen genuinely, and still move things forward.

### What the interviewer wants to hear

They want to see empathy, shared goals, and a constructive resolution. They are watching to see if you badmouth others, if you take all the credit, or if you understand that conflict in a workplace is almost always about differences in priority or information, not personality.

### How to answer it

Follow the STAR structure, and make sure your answer hits these four beats:

1. **Empathy first** - Show that before jumping to solutions, you took the time to understand where the other person was coming from. What were their concerns? What pressures were they facing?
2. **Shared goals** - Frame the story around a common objective. You and the other person both wanted the project to succeed. The conflict was about how, not why.
3. **Your action** - What did you do to bridge the gap? How did you move the conversation forward without dismissing the other person's perspective?
4. **Result and takeaway** - What was the outcome, and what did you learn? A strong answer ties the lesson back to how you work differently now because of it.

Choose a story with real stakes. A conflict about a trivial decision will not land. The interviewer should hear your story and think "that was a genuinely difficult situation." That is how you leave an impression.

**Variations of this question to prepare for:**
- "Tell me about a time you had to manage a difficult stakeholder."
- "Describe a situation where you disagreed with your manager."
- "How do you handle working with someone whose style clashes with yours?"

### Example

"I was working on a data pipeline project where my team proposed switching to real-time streaming using Apache Kafka to fix a performance issue we were dealing with. The engineering leadership understood the value of the solution, but they were concerned about the implementation timeline since we were already blocked on other deliverables. Rather than pushing back, I first sat down with the relevant stakeholders to understand their constraints more clearly. Their concern was not about the solution itself, it was about risk and timing. So we came up with a two-pronged approach: a short-term fix to unblock the immediate issue, and a parallel track to begin the Kafka implementation at a manageable pace. We also reached out to sister teams who had already done similar implementations so we could compress our research timeline. Everyone walked away with their concerns addressed, and we delivered the long-term fix ahead of schedule."

## Question 4: "How would you convince a stakeholder or your manager to go with your approach?"

### Why they ask it

This is a more nuanced version of the conflict question. Here, the interviewer is looking specifically at your ability to influence without authority, which is a core skill in any cross-functional role. They want to know if you can make a compelling case for your ideas without bulldozing others.

### What the interviewer wants to hear

They want someone who leads with logic and data, not ego. Someone who ties their proposal to business outcomes and is open to testing or iterating when there is genuine uncertainty.

### How to answer it

Three approaches work well here, and you can combine all three in your answer:

1. **Tie your solution to shared goals** - Show how your approach serves the company, the customer, or the project's core objective. This removes the "my idea vs your idea" framing and replaces it with "what is best for the outcome."
2. **Speak with data** - Bring numbers into the conversation. Data removes subjectivity. If your solution has a measurable advantage over the alternative, show it. If you can compare both approaches side by side, even better.
3. **Propose an experiment** - If the conversation is at a stalemate, suggest testing both approaches at a smaller scale. This signals that you are confident in your solution, open-minded, and not afraid of being proven wrong.

### Example

"In my role, I often work with stakeholders who have different views on how to prioritise features. When I believe strongly in a particular approach, I try to lead with the business case first. I frame it around what we are trying to achieve for the customer or the business, then I back it up with whatever data I have available, whether that is from past experiments, user research, or industry benchmarks. If there is still genuine disagreement, I usually suggest running a smaller test so we can let the results speak rather than continuing to debate. That kind of approach tends to move people because it shows confidence without being dismissive of their concerns."

## Handling Questions You Were Not Prepared For

This will happen to everyone at some point, including experienced candidates. Here is how to handle it without panicking.

**Take the pause.** You do not need to answer the moment the question leaves the interviewer's mouth. It is completely acceptable to say "That is a great question, let me take a few seconds to gather my thoughts" and then actually do it. Candidates who rush into answers without thinking often give worse answers than those who pause deliberately.

**Repurpose a story you already have.** If you have a bank of 6 to 8 strong stories, most unexpected questions can be addressed by adapting one of them. Take a moment, identify the closest story in your bank, and frame it around what the question is asking.

**If you genuinely do not have a story, say so honestly and pivot.** It is better to say "I have not encountered that exact situation, but here is how I would approach it based on my experience with similar challenges" than to fabricate something unconvincing. Interviewers often respect the honesty, especially if your hypothetical answer is thoughtful and structured.

**Remember that storytelling is everything.** A candidate who fumbles slightly but tells a vivid, well-structured story will be remembered more fondly than someone who delivers a polished but hollow answer. The interviewer takes away the story, not the pauses.

## Quick Reference: The 3 Things Every Behavioural Answer Needs

| Element | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Context | A real situation with real stakes, not something trivial |
| Your specific action | What YOU did, not what the team did generically |
| Result and takeaway | A measurable outcome and what you learned from it |

## Building Your Story Bank

Before any interview, sit down and prepare written answers for at least the following themes. Every behavioural question you will ever be asked maps back to one of these:

- A time you handled competing priorities
- A conflict with a team member or stakeholder
- A time you failed or made a mistake
- A time you had to influence someone without direct authority
- A time you went above and beyond
- A time you dealt with ambiguity
- A time you had to learn something quickly
- A time you led a project or initiative

Write out the full STAR story for each one. Practice saying them out loud, not to memorise them word for word, but so the structure feels natural. When you walk into the interview, you will not be starting from scratch. You will be choosing which story fits best and then telling it well.
